The Urgency of Climate Action

The changing climate poses a profound threat to humankind, disrupting ecosystems and intensifying natural disasters. As temperatures rise and weather patterns shift, food and water security are compromised. To safeguard our future, collective action and sustainable practices are essential, fostering resilience and ensuring a livable planet.

History of Climate Change

The story of climate change and its link to greenhouse gas emissions in the United States is not just about science; it's about how we’ve learned, struggled, and sometimes failed to protect our planet while advancing human progress. This tale begins long before "climate change" became a household term, but it’s woven deeply into the history of industrialization, scientific discovery, and modern policy debates.

The seeds of our understanding of climate change were planted in the 19th century. In 1856, a remarkable woman named Eunice Newton Foote made a startling observation: she hypothesized that certain gases, especially carbon dioxide (CO₂), had the ability to trap heat in the atmosphere. She wasn’t the only one thinking along these lines. About 40 years later, Svante Arrhenius, a Swedish chemist, took this idea further. He connected the burning of fossil fuels—coal, oil, and gas—to the warming of the Earth’s atmosphere. But this was still theory, and few paid attention to the idea that human activity might be altering the planet’s climate.

Fast forward to the late 19th and early 20th centuries—the era of the Industrial Revolution. Factories roared to life, powered by coal, and later oil. The United States, being one of the global leaders in industrial output, was pumping out massive amounts of CO₂. At this point, no one truly understood the environmental cost. The focus was on production, progress, and power. But behind the scenes, the invisible blanket of greenhouse gases was thickening around the planet, trapping more and more heat.

The 1950s marked a turning point. Up until then, the idea of human-driven climate change was more speculation than fact. But that changed thanks to Charles David Keeling, a quiet scientist who began carefully measuring CO₂ levels in the atmosphere from Hawaii’s Mauna Loa Observatory. His measurements showed a steady rise in CO₂ levels year after year, and he produced what’s now called the Keeling Curve. This was one of the first solid pieces of evidence that something was happening—humans were indeed affecting the planet's atmosphere in ways no one had fully grasped before.

   

By the 1970s, Americans were becoming more conscious of environmental issues. Smog choked cities like Los Angeles, rivers were so polluted they could catch fire, and public health was visibly suffering. These problems forced the government to act, leading to the creation of the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) in 1970 and the passing of the Clean Air Act. While these laws primarily targeted air pollution and industrial waste, they also laid the groundwork for addressing climate change later on. However, at that time, the term "climate change" wasn’t even part of public discourse. People were more concerned about dirty rivers than rising temperatures.

The 1980s were a critical decade. The scientific community began ringing alarm bells more loudly about the dangers of rising greenhouse gases. In 1988, the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) was established by the United Nations to assess the risks posed by climate change. By this point, there was growing consensus: human activities, particularly the burning of fossil fuels, were increasing global temperatures. In the U.S., greenhouse gas emissions were still climbing, fueled by a growing economy and a love affair with cars and cheap energy. Yet, the political will to act was weak. The focus was still on economic growth rather than curbing emissions.

The 1990s brought the issue of climate change onto the global stage. In 1997, the world came together to sign the Kyoto Protocol, the first international agreement aimed at reducing greenhouse gas emissions. For the first time, developed nations, including the U.S., were expected to lead the way in cutting emissions. But here’s where things got complicated. The U.S. Congress refused to ratify the agreement, arguing that it placed an unfair economic burden on the country and did not require developing nations to cut emissions.

The early 2000s saw climate change thrust into the public eye, thanks in part to Al Gore’s documentary, An Inconvenient Truth (2006). Gore’s film, filled with stark visuals of melting ice caps and flooded cities, made climate change personal. The message was clear: this wasn’t some distant problem—it was happening now, and human actions were to blame. Meanwhile, in 2009, the EPA declared greenhouse gases a threat to public health, giving it the authority to regulate emissions under the Clean Air Act. The Obama administration took steps to tackle emissions, introducing policies like the Clean Power Plan, aimed at reducing emissions from coal-fired power plants. But these efforts faced fierce opposition from industry groups and political factions who saw environmental regulations as a threat to the economy.

In 2015, nearly every country in the world signed the Paris Agreement, a landmark treaty to limit global warming to well below 2°C, with aspirations of keeping it to 1.5°C. For the U.S., this was a big moment—finally, the country was committing to meaningful climate action. But in 2017, the Trump administration pulled the U.S. out of the agreement, arguing that it was unfair and would hurt the American economy. This flip-flop in U.S. policy reflected the larger debate within the country—how to balance economic interests with environmental responsibility. Despite the federal government’s retreat, many states, cities, and corporations continued their efforts to reduce emissions and invest in clean energy.

In 2021, under the Biden administration, the U.S. rejoined the Paris Agreement. The new administration has pledged to reduce U.S. greenhouse gas emissions by 50-52% below 2005 levels by 2030 and achieve net-zero emissions by 2050. This is an ambitious goal, but the challenges are enormous. The U.S. still heavily relies on fossil fuels, and transitioning to renewable energy sources—like wind, solar, and electric vehicles—requires massive investments and political will. At the same time, methane, a potent greenhouse gas, has become a growing concern. While CO₂ dominates the headlines, methane’s short-term impact on warming is far more intense. Reducing methane emissions, particularly from oil and gas operations and agriculture, is seen as a key part of the U.S.'s climate strategy. Currently, sea levels are rising rapidly. This can also lead to a global catastrophe, so humanity needs to solve these problems.

The history of climate change, especially through the lens of U.S. greenhouse gas emissions, is a story of scientific breakthroughs, political battles, and societal change. It’s also a story that’s still unfolding. As the climate crisis worsens—with more extreme weather, rising sea levels, and shifting ecosystems—the actions we take today will shape the future of our planet for generations to come.
